The Manual — read this top to bottom

This machine is Jorgensen's whole theory of the golf swing: the golfer is reduced to two rigid rods. Rod A (drawn as the red lead arm) runs from a hub between the shoulders down to the wrists. Rod C (the club) hangs off it at a hinge — the wrists. The golfer can do exactly three things: twist the arms about the hub, slide the hub sideways with the hips, and decide what the wrist hinge does. Everything else — the legs, the head, the trail arm — is drawn only to make the robot look like a person; it carries no physics. The black dots are the clubhead photographed by a strobe every 5 milliseconds, exactly like the photograph on the book's cover. The faint blue dots are Jorgensen's measured professional — the standard swing — left on the plate for comparison. Change any slider and the downswing is recomputed from the equations of motion instantly.

Inputs — the knobs

Preset swings
Canned experiments from the book. Standard swing is the fitted professional (111 mph). No shift and No gravity switch off one energy source each. Casting spends the wrists at the top; Hold-off saves them; Zero wrist-cock swings stiff-wristed with a longer backswing; Short compact backswing chops 30° off γ; Sunday morning is a gentle old-man swing.
Shoulder torque TS (lb·ft)
The steady twist the big muscles apply to the arms for the whole downswing — the engine of the swing. Jorgensen measured about 57 lb·ft for his professional. Doubling your effort disappoints: +5% torque buys only ~2% clubhead speed, because a faster swing leaves less time for the torque to do work.
Backswing angle γ (deg)
How far the arms are wound back from the ball at the top. 165.8° means the hands are up past vertical. A longer backswing gives the torque a longer runway. This is where the real distance lives: the last 30° of backswing are worth more than any amount of wrist effort.
Wrist-cock at top β(0) (deg)
The angle folded between the club and the line of the arms at the top. The pro carries about 124°. This is the famous lag: energy parked in a folded hinge, to be paid out late in the swing where it multiplies clubhead speed.
Arm length R (ft)
Shoulder axis to wrists, swung as one rigid rod — 2.2 ft for the pro. Longer arms sweep a bigger circle for the same turn.
Arm weight (lb)
The effective weight of both arms treated as that single rod. Heavier arms store more momentum but are harder to accelerate with the same torque.
Gravity g (ft/s²)
Earth is 32.2. Set it to zero and you lose about 8% of clubhead speed — gravity is free energy: the arms and club simply fall from the top, and a good swing banks that fall. (Yes, this slider lets you golf on the Moon.)
Pull toward target (ft/s²)
The shift: the hips drag the whole hub — the center of the swing — sideways toward the target as the downswing starts. The pro's hub accelerates at 47.4 ft/s² (about 1.5 g). Worth roughly 10% of clubhead speed, which is why teachers say the downswing starts from the ground up.
Pull duration (s)
How long that pull lasts before the slide must be stopped — 0.16 s for the pro, roughly two-thirds of the downswing.
Braking push (ft/s²)
The firm lead leg. The hub is decelerated (50.6 ft/s² for the pro), and — like cracking a whip — braking the bottom of the system slings the top forward. Slide without brake wastes the shift.
Club length, wrist→head (ft)
From the wrist-hinge point (about 5 in below the grip end) to the clubhead — 3.5 ft for a driver. Longer club, bigger circle, more speed — and more inertia to drag around.
Clubhead weight (oz)
The mass concentrated at the far end. It fights you twice: it slows the swing down, and at impact it carries the momentum into the ball. There is a sweet spot; giant heads on this slider swing like sledgehammers.
Shaft weight (oz)
Mass spread along the shaft. Cheaper to carry than head weight (half of it rides near your hands), but it still costs speed. This is why real shafts went graphite.
Natural release vs forced at angle
The book's central discovery. Natural: the wrists merely hold the cock angle until the centrifugal pull of the swinging club wants to throw it open — the holding torque falls to zero — and then they simply let go. The club releases itself; no hit is required. Forced at angle lets you choose the arm angle at which the hinge opens, so you can test early or deliberately late releases.
Extra wrist torque TE (lb·ft)
Muscle applied at the hinge on top of the hold. Positive = casting, hitting from the top: the duffer's move, spends the lag early and usually loses speed. Negative = holding off: fighting to keep the lag longer — the "late hit" the pros are accused of. A couple of lb·ft either way is all human wrists are worth.
TE duration (s)
How long that extra wrist muscle acts, counted from the start of the downswing. Jorgensen's experiments used 0.10 s — about the length of Phase I, before the club starts to throw itself open.

Outputs — the dials

Clubhead speed at impact (mph, ft/s)
The number the whole machine exists to produce: how fast the clubhead is moving when the arms reach the ball. The standard swing delivers about 111 mph. The green/red percentage compares your swing to that professional.
Ball leaves at (mph)
What the ball does with that clubhead speed, using the book's collision physics: coefficient of restitution e = 0.7 and ball-to-clubhead mass ratio 0.19 give a smash factor of 1.43 — the ball leaves 43% faster than the clubhead that struck it.
Wrist-cock at impact β(i) (deg)
How much hinge is still folded when the ball is struck. The pro arrives with ~15° left — the wrists never fully unload, and it costs almost nothing (the last 30° of uncocking are worth ~4% of speed). Large β(i): you released too late or the club is too sluggish. Negative: the clubhead overtook the hands — a flip.
Downswing duration (ms)
Top of backswing to impact — about a quarter of a second for the pro. There is no time in here to think; that is the point of the whole book.
Wrists let go at (ms)
The moment the holding torque crossed zero and the hinge opened (~90 ms, when the arms are still well above horizontal). Everything after this is ballistic — the golfer is a passenger.
Hub shift at impact (in)
How far the hips have carried the swing center toward the target by the time the ball is struck — about 14 inches for the pro, matching what you see in film of good players.
Kinetic energy delivered (ft·lb)
The energy of the whole moving system at impact — arms, club, and slide together. Around 235 ft·lb for the standard swing: the work of lifting ~30 lb about eight feet, spent in a quarter of a second — roughly two horsepower.
Peak clubhead speed (mph)
The fastest the clubhead ever got. If this exceeds the impact number, the swing peaked before the ball — an early release wasting speed into thin air. In a well-timed swing the peak IS the impact.
Chart: clubhead speed vs time
The S-curve of the downswing (the book's Fig. 2.2). Slow build while the wrists stay cocked, then the steep payoff as the club unfolds. Red is your swing; dashed blue is the standard swing; the dotted vertical is the release; the dashed horizontal is the pro's 163.4 ft/s benchmark.
Chart: wrist-cock β vs time
The lag ledger (the book's Fig. 2.4). Flat at first — the angle is merely held — then it dives as centrifugal force throws the club open on its own. Casting tilts the flat part down early; holding off keeps it flat longer.
The strobe plate
Black dots: your clubhead every 5 ms — where dots crowd together the club is slow, where they spread it is fast (all the spreading happens at the bottom; that is the whole secret). Small red dots: your hands. Blue dots: the pro's clubhead. Faint grey spokes: the bare two-rod skeleton of the theory, drawn every 20 ms. The IMPACT stamp appears when the arms reach the ball.
The model is Jorgensen's: two rigid rods — the arms swung by a constant torque about a hub that may shift toward the target, the club hinged at the wrists — moving in the inclined plane of the swing. The wrists hold the cock angle until the holding torque falls to zero, then the club releases itself; no wrist “hit” is needed. The dashed curve is his standard swing, fitted to a stroboscopic photograph of a driver swung at 163.4 ft/s (111 mph): TS = 57 lb·ft, γ = 165.8°, β(0) = 124.2°, shift +47.4 ft/s² for 0.16 s then −50.6 ft/s². Sources: lib553 chapters 2–5 and Technical Appendices 4–5. Calculated swings are instructive, not exact — Jorgensen's own caveat.
